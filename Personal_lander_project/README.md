This project is a physics-based simulation of a lunar powered descent, built from scratch in Python as a personal learning exercise. The spacecraft starts on a 100 km circular parking orbit around the Moon, performs a deorbit burn, and then executes a fully autonomous landing using a three-phase PID controller.

Physics engine. The equations of motion are integrated in a rotating Moon-fixed spherical frame with state vector [r, lat, lon, v_r, v_e, v_n, mass] using a variable-step RK45 integrator. Thrust is a full 3-D vector [T_r, T_e, T_n] applied independently to the radial, eastward, and northward velocity channels, with mass depletion computed from the rocket equation at each timestep. All physical constants (lunar gravity, radius, rotation rate) and vehicle parameters (dry mass, propellant, max thrust, Isp) are defined in YAML config files under config/.

Guidance and control. Landing is managed by a three-phase PID controller. In Phase 1 (altitude above 2000 m), a proportional term brakes all three velocity components simultaneously, cancelling the ~1633 m/s orbital horizontal speed gradually and producing a smooth, Apollo-style curved arc rather than a hard right-angle turn. In Phase 2 (100 to 2000 m), a PID loop with anti-windup takes over vertical speed control, driving the descent rate to a reference profile. In Phase 3 (below 100 m), proportional horizontal damping is added on top of the vertical PID to null any residual lateral drift, achieving touchdown at under 2 m/s vertical and under 0.1 m/s horizontal.

Visualisation. Running mission.py launches two sequential viewers. The first is a 3-D textured Moon globe (PyVista) showing the full mission: the circular parking orbit in blue, the deorbit point, and the complete descent arc in orange down to the touchdown site. Close this window to proceed. The second is a close-up animation of the final 300 m of descent: a gold 3-D lander model orients dynamically along the instantaneous thrust vector frame-by-frame, an orange trail traces the path, and a smooth cyan ring marks the 20 m landing target on the surface. Live telemetry (time, altitude, vertical speed, horizontal speed, thrust-to-weight ratio, and remaining propellant mass) is overlaid in the corner. Matplotlib plots of the full trajectory and controller history are also generated and saved automatically.
